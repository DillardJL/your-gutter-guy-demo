Fascia Board Repair in Salem, VA & the Roanoke Valley

Rotted or damaged fascia boards are the hidden reason gutters sag, pull away, and fail to hold. Jeff repairs and replaces fascia before re-hanging gutters — ensuring your whole system is properly supported and built to last.

What Is Fascia?

Fascia is the horizontal board that runs along the lower edge of your roof, directly behind the gutters. It's what the gutters are actually nailed or screwed into — every gutter hanger bracket anchors to the fascia, which means the fascia is carrying the full weight of the gutter system, including any water or debris that accumulates inside.

Beyond supporting the gutters, fascia plays several other important roles in your home's structure and appearance:

✓
Seals the roofline from weather and pests. The fascia closes the gap between the bottom edge of the roof decking and the exterior of the house. When it's intact, it blocks rain, insects, and small animals from getting into the roof cavity or attic space. When it rots through, those entry points open up.
✓
Provides a finished appearance to the roofline. Fascia is a visible part of your home's exterior. A stained, cracked, or visibly rotted fascia board detracts from curb appeal and signals deferred maintenance to anyone who looks closely — including potential buyers.
✓
Supports gutter weight and alignment. Gutters can hold a surprising amount of weight when full of water or wet debris. The fascia distributes that load along the roofline. If the fascia softens from moisture, gutter hangers pull through the wood rather than holding firm — and gutters begin to sag or separate from the house.

The most common cause of fascia damage is prolonged moisture exposure — either from clogged gutters that overflow and soak the wood behind them, or from gutters that have already begun to pull away and allow water to run directly down the fascia face. Once rot sets in, it spreads and can compromise the soffit and roof decking as well if not addressed.

Signs of Fascia Damage

Fascia damage isn't always obvious from the ground, but there are clear indicators to watch for. Some signs show up on the fascia itself; others appear in the behavior of your gutters or the surrounding materials.

  • ✓
    Sagging or pulling gutters. When fascia can no longer hold the weight of the gutter, hangers pull loose and sections begin to droop or separate from the house entirely. If you've had a contractor try to re-hang gutters that immediately sag again, rotted fascia is the likely culprit — new hangers can't grip softened wood.
  • ✓
    Visible rot or soft spots. Fascia that has been wet for extended periods will feel soft when pressed, show visible discoloration or dark staining, and may have areas where the wood has started to crumble or delaminate. Even if the paint appears intact on the surface, the wood underneath may be badly compromised.
  • ✓
    Peeling paint on the fascia board. Paint that bubbles or peels specifically on the fascia (rather than the siding or trim generally) is a sign of moisture moving through the wood from behind. This often indicates that water is getting in at the gutter-fascia interface — either from overflow or from water backing up under the gutter lip.
  • ✓
    Water stains below the roofline. Dark streaks or staining on the siding below the fascia line — especially in areas away from downspout locations — suggest water is running down the face of the fascia and siding rather than being captured by the gutter system.
  • ✓
    Pest entry points. Rotted fascia creates gaps and soft spots that wasps, carpenter bees, and small animals use to enter the roof cavity. If you've noticed stinging insect activity near the roofline or heard animal movement in the attic or soffit area, damaged fascia may be providing the access point.
  • ✓
    Gutters separating from the house. A gutter that has pulled visibly away from the roofline — with a visible gap between the top of the gutter and the roof edge — is almost always a sign that the fascia behind it has failed. The gutter is trying to stay attached to something that can no longer hold it.

Fascia Repair & Replacement

Jeff handles fascia repair and replacement as a standalone service and as part of larger gutter projects. The approach depends on the extent of the damage and the condition of the surrounding materials.

Partial Repair

When damage is localized to one section — a single run between corners, or a discrete area around a downspout outlet — the damaged portion can be cut out and replaced without disturbing the rest of the fascia. This is the most cost-effective approach when the problem hasn't spread. Jeff checks adjacent sections carefully to make sure the repair addresses all compromised wood.

Full Run Replacement

When rot has spread along a full section of the roofline, or when the fascia is old and multiple sections are soft or deteriorating, replacing the full run is the cleaner and more durable solution. This ensures the new gutters have solid, continuous backing along their entire length — no weak spots that will cause problems within a few seasons.

Always Done Before Gutter Work

Fascia repair is consistently handled before new gutters are installed or existing gutters are re-hung. Hanging gutters on compromised fascia is a short-term fix — the hangers won't hold, the gutters will sag again, and the underlying rot will continue to worsen. Jeff will not install or re-hang gutters on fascia that can't properly support them. If fascia work is needed, he'll identify it during the estimate and include it in the project plan.

Fascia replacement material is typically primed wood board or a composite material, matched to the existing profile and painted to blend with your trim color. Jeff will discuss material options with you based on what's already on the house and what makes sense for durability and budget.

Common Questions About Fascia Repair

How do I know if my fascia needs repair?⌄

The clearest signs are sagging gutters that won't stay re-hung, water damage visible on or directly below the roofline, and visible rot or soft wood on the fascia board itself. From the ground, look for gutters that have visibly separated from the house or sections of trim that look warped, stained dark, or have paint that's bubbling and peeling in a specific band along the roofline edge. If you're unsure, call Jeff for an estimate — he'll get on the ladder and tell you exactly what's there.

Does fascia repair need to be done before new gutters are installed?⌄

Yes — without exception. Gutters attach directly to the fascia via hanger brackets. If the fascia is soft or rotted, the hangers will pull through the wood within a season or two (or sooner, under heavy rain or ice load). Repairing or replacing the fascia first ensures the new gutters have a sound, solid surface to anchor to. It's not an upsell — it's a necessary part of doing the job correctly so the gutters actually stay where they're supposed to.

What material do you use for fascia replacement?⌄

Replacement fascia is typically primed wood board or a composite (PVC or fiber cement) material, depending on what's already on the house and the homeowner's preference. Primed wood matched to the existing profile is the most common choice — it's cost-effective, easy to paint, and holds up well when properly maintained. Composite options are available for situations where long-term moisture resistance is a priority. Jeff will walk through the options during the estimate based on your specific situation.
